# Target Audience

## Core demographic

- **Ages 26–44**, with **34% concentrated in the 28–38 range** (the densest core).
- Both men and women.
- Any kind of romantic relationship (male-female, male-male, female-female).
- Comfortable with mobile apps and AI tools.
- Likely partnered for at least a year — Entie is most useful to couples who already have shared history.

## Primary pain points

These are the three core pains Entie addresses. Marketing copy should speak to at least one of them.

### 1. Weak emotional connection

People (especially women) feel emotionally disconnected from their partner and don't know how to close the gap. They love their partner but don't feel *seen*. Entie helps surface what's actually going on.

### 2. Fading passion

Passion erodes over years of being together, and couples don't have a vocabulary or a tool to address it. They notice the change but feel embarrassed to name it. Entie gives them a private, low-stakes way to work on it.

### 3. No shared direction

Partners don't share a clear sense of where they're headed as a couple or what they're building toward. Day-to-day life takes over and the bigger picture goes unspoken. Couple Questions and Guided Space create space for that conversation.

## Audience segments to keep in mind

When writing copy, picture one of these segments:

- **The couple who wants to strengthen their relationship** — proactive, growth-minded, often early in a long-term commitment.
- **Married people who want to deepen their bond** — past the honeymoon, looking for tools that fit a long marriage.
- **Couples who want to share schedules** — practical-minded, want sync and visibility into each other's lives.
- **Women who want to track their cycle** — solo cycle trackers who may or may not share data.
- **Couples who want to share cycle data** — modern partners who treat the cycle as a shared awareness, not a private chore.
- **Any partnered adult in a relationship** — broadly, anyone who wants help showing up better for their partner.

## What this audience responds to

- Privacy framed as respect, not as a security feature
- Real moments over abstract claims ("Tuesday after work, when she's quiet" beats "improve communication")
- Soft humor about the universal awkwardness of being in love long-term
- Equality of effort — neither partner is positioned as the one who needs fixing

## What this audience does *not* respond to

- "Self-help" language
- Therapy-speak ("attachment styles," "love languages" — overused)
- Productivity framing ("optimize your relationship")
- Anything that hints at competition between partners
- Gendered assumptions about who does the emotional work
